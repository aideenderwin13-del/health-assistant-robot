 If had unlimited time, skills, and resources, I would love to design a robot. During my time on the Irish Robotics Team, I didn’t have a role directly connected to hardware or robot programming, but I always found it incredibly interesting. One idea I came up with was a health assistance robot.
During the summer, my best friend underwent spinal fusion surgery due to scoliosis, and the main challenge she faced was recovery. Being from the Midlands in a rural setting, it was difficult to stay in regular contact with her doctors and consultants. This made me think, instead of people always having to travel to doctors, why not bring the doctors to them?
My concept would monitor everyday health factors such as posture, movement, activity levels, and daily routines. Rather than diagnosing or treating illness, it would focus on awareness and prevention by identifying patterns such as long periods of inactivity, poor posture, or irregular habits. The robot would provide gentle reminders or suggestions, encouraging movement, stretching, or rest.
The robot would use cameras, motion sensors, and AI to observe how a person moves throughout the day. Over time, it would learn what “normal” looks like for each individual and detect changes or unhealthy trends. If anything concerning occurred, the robot would flag this information and share it with a doctor or medical professional for review.
The robot wouldn’t feel like a medical device, but rather a supportive presence in the background. Its goal wouldn’t be to scold the user, but to make recovery easier and less stressful. While this isn’t a field I would usually pursue, this question pushed me to think about how I could design something that benefits not only myself, but others as well.
Robots are currently used around the world in many different settings, yet there is still a lack of solutions addressing the challenge of limited access to healthcare services in rural areas. This idea isn’t just practical, it has the potential to genuinely change lives, especially when hospital appointments can be expensive, not including transport, accommodation, or additional medical costs.
